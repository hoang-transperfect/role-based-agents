## Process
Requirements Gathering Process - Step by Step for a BA.
Step 1: Identify Stakeholders
Recognize all individuals and groups affected by the project.
Examples: end-users, project sponsors, SMEs, developers, etc.

Step 2: Plan Requirements Gathering
Define objectives, scope, and methods for gathering requirements.
Choose appropriate techniques (interviews, workshops, surveys, etc.).
Schedule and prepare for sessions.

Step 3: Conduct Elicitation Sessions
Use selected techniques to extract requirements:
- Interviews - one-on-one discussions
- Workshops - collaborative group sessions
- Surveys/Questionnaires - gather broad input quickly
- Observation - watch how users interact with current systems
- Document Analysis - review existing documentation

Step 4: Document Observations and Findings
Capture key inputs and insights from sessions.
Use structured formats: meeting notes, whiteboard photos, audio transcripts.

Step 5: Analyse and Validate Requirements
Organize, interpret, and confirm accuracy of requirements.
Use tools such as:
- MoSCoW prioritization (Must, Should, Could, Won't)
- Kano Model - evaluates customer satisfaction
- SWOT, Root Cause Analysis (ifsatisfaction)

Step 6: Prioritize Requirements
Rank requirements based on value, urgency, feasibility, and alignment with business goals.
Engage stakeholders to agree on what is most important.

Step 7: Document Requirements
Create formal documentation:
- Business Requirements Document (BRD)
- Functional Requirements Document (FRD)
- User Stories / Use Cases
  Ensure clarity, traceability, and testability.

Step 8: Review & Get Stakeholder Sign-Off
Present documented requirements to stakeholders.
Clarify ambiguities, correct errors.
Get formal approval/sign-off to move forward.

Step 9: Manage Changes & Maintain Traceability
Use a Requirements Traceability Matrix (RTM).
Track requirement changes, status updates, and dependencies.
Ensure alignment with project goals throughout the lifecycle.

One addition that could strengthen this even further: continuous stakeholder engagement beyond sign-off. Too often, requirements are "locked" early, but evolving understanding and changing needs mean we should be validating and adjusting throughout delivery.

Also worth mentioning:
✅ Context framing — understanding the business problem before gathering requirements avoids building the wrong thing beautifully
✅ Non-functional requirements — performance, usability, accessibility, and security often get missed but are just as critical
✅ Service-level perspective — not all requirements are about features; some are about end-to-end journeys, governance, or support models
